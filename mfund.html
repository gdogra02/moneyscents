Mutual Funds: Shorting the Future?


March 22, 2017 by Roger Nath


“Mutual funds? What are those?”, I wondered as I scrolled through an Investopedia article some years back. Indeed, the concept is counterintuitive, and perhaps even downright confusing.


At its core, a mutual fund is a professionally managed investment vehicle consisting of a pool of funds collected from many investors for the purpose of investing in securities like stocks, bonds, and similar assets. Each investor shares in the gains/loss of the fund proportionate to the size of their investment.


In essence, the fee structure is such that money/portfolio managers (those allocating the money in the fund) receive a performance based fee based on capital gains (i.e.- what they make through investing), and more counter-intuitively, a service fee of sorts solely for managing the money (regardless of their performance).


Historically, mutual funds have been designed and cautiously regulated to provide a decent risk-adjusted rate of return on the principle of hundreds of thousands of Canadians like you and me (well my parents anyways). For decades, those looking to buy a bigger house, fund their kids’ tuition, or pay for a special vacation down the road, would turn to mutual funds. However, the consensus among many is that this is changing… here’s why and what it means for you.


According to the Huffington Post, the average total cost of investing in a “balanced” Canadian mutual fund is 2.63%. While this may not seem like a lot, it actually represents of some of the highest fees in the world (despite fees decreasing to rationalize worse performance). Think about this: someone with a portfolio of $500,000 will pay $13,150 per year alone in fees- sometimes more depending on the fund.


“Ok, but doesn’t the performance of the fund justify the fee?”, you might be asking. Well… not exactly. For the fiscal year ending in 2015, according to McGraw Hill Financial, 70% or more of Canadian funds underperformed the S&P/TSX composite (the market) over the last five years. Interestingly, statistically there is an inverse relationship between the fee and fund performance; the higher the fee the worse the performance. Perhaps the idea of more cost for better result is not so in the mutual fund industry. Though it is hard to beat the market when funds are managing hundreds of millions or even billions of dollars, investors do still expect better-than-current performance…


Of course, this doesn’t apply to all mutual funds. BlackRock and Vanguard’s passive management style, low-cost products, and ETFs have allowed them to prosper over actively managed funds like Pimco, and Franklin Templeton as of late. In fact, Vanguard’s assets have climbed 7.2% to $3.1 trillion from 2014-2015.


As hinted in the last paragraphs, perhaps the most fiercely competing investment vehicles are those of ETFs- Exchange Traded Funds. These are marketable securities comprised of investment funds traded on stock exchanges (much like stocks). They hold/track assets such as stocks, commodities, bonds, index funds (basket of assets), and trade close to their net asset value over.


Furthermore, unprecedented, increased access to information and services has made it easier than ever for truly driven individuals to “learn the ropes” of finance, and manage their own investment/trading portfolios, further competing with mutual funds.


Lastly, another large threat stems from the advent of the Artificially Intelligent robo-advisor/manager. These are online-based wealth management services which provide automated, algorithm-based portfolio management/advice with no human financial planners, all the while providing similar (if not better) returns over the long-term. They’re typically low-cost, have low account minimums, and particularly popular among younger investors (myself included). Popular ones include Canada’s own Quest-Trade’s Portfolio IQ and Wealthsimple (the latter with $750M under management as of December 2016).


While one can’t say with certainty (especially a 15-year-old like myself) that mutual funds are a thing of the past, unless they make some innovations and adaptations to their current business models (i.e.- not unlike those of Vanguard and Blackrock), that may very well be the case. Only time will tell….
