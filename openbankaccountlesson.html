Opening a Bank Account


Personal Banking: Lesson 1


Introduction


Opening a bank account as a teen for the first time can be a confusing and overwhelming experience. While a parent or other form of support with more experience may be able to help in the process, this lesson (and the following quiz) should help much of your own understanding and learning involved in the process.


Part I: Ensuring Eligibility


The first step you should take in this process is making sure that you’re eligible to make an account. As a general rule of thumb, most banks look for the following criteria:


  1. If you’re under 18, you and your parents will likely have to sign some forms before setting up an account (though not at all banks so make sure you inquire about this)

  2. You’ll certainly need to have valid ID and be able to present basic information about yourself for record purposes (e.g. - home address, phone-number, DOB, etc.)

  3. You’ll need to have a minimum amount of money to setup the account; usually a couple hundred dollars (though varies from bank to bank)

Making sure you meet these basic criteria (and more depending on the bank - do your research!) is essential to being able to open an account, and can make the process much more efficient if known ahead of time.


Part II: Choosing the Right Bank


After establishing whether or not you’re eligible to even open an account, the next step is to choose the bank that is right for you. Of course, not all banks are the same, even when it comes to personal accounts, so doing the appropriate research is absolutely imperative.


While all banks are different, one general categorization is clear: large, chain banks and smaller chain/singular/local banks.


Large chain banks (e.g. - CIBC, Scotiabank, RBC, etc.) typically have branches in towns and cities across countries, making access, convenience, and service relatively uniform almost everywhere. Reduced likelihood of having to use other banks’ ATMs, 24 hour help-lines, and a stable/trusted reputation are features that you can expect from larger chain banks.


Smaller-banks on the other hand likely don’t have the resources or infrastructure to provide as many or as accessible products/services as larger ones, but likely offer a more personal experience and potentially better customer service and experience. This may translate into their products and services themselves: waiving certain fees, levying more lenient rewards programs for more loyal customers, and more. They also usually charge lower fees, but are also more likely to fail than larger banks.


Part III: Picking the Right Account


The vast majority of the time people open either a chequing or savings account (or both). Each account allows you to safely store money in an account with a bank and withdraw it when it’s needed. However, either account has some unique features which should be highlighted:


Chequing: This account is used primarily for frequent transactions on a daily basis, like buying milk from a convenience store. With this account, you’ll receive a check book and a debit card to use for transactions. Moreover, unlike savings accounts, chequing accounts don’t accrue interest - the money you put in is constant and won’t change unless you deposit more of it.


Savings: Savings account are best employed for saving money over the long-term. They accrue interest slowly at a very low rate (e.g. - 0.1%), and the more money and length of time you store, the more interest you will gain from the bank. Like a chequing account, you can still withdraw money from a savings account at banks and ATMs, but you can’t generally use savings accounts for checks and debit card payments.


Ideally, if you have the minimum amount to setup either account, having both would be ideal. You could use the chequing account for daily expenditures, and put extra-money in savings accounts to bear interest.


Part IV: Visiting the Bank


Now that you have a basic knowledge about the process and some of the details behind opening a bank account, it’s finally time to visit the bank to formally open the account.


While doing so online and over the form may be an option for some banks, going in-person is typically the best option for first-time account holders. In addition, you can ask tellers about any questions you may still have before finalizing (e.g. - What are the monthly fees if any? What is my interest rate on savings account? Is there a transaction limit?, etc.) and receive immediate and accurate feedback. Moreover, the process is usually quicker as forms and documents can be signed, received, and verified on the spot.


Part V: Supplying the Necessary Information


More than likely, you’ll need to provide documentation to confirm the information needed in order to setup your account. While this depends precisely on the specific bank account that you wish to open, in general, it is a good idea to have:


  1. Government-issued ID with your photo on it (e.g.- driver’s License or passport)

  2. Proof of address (e.g. - phone bill, driver’s license, etc.)

  3. Proof of registered citizenship (e.g.- Social Security Number, taxpayer ID number, employer ID number, etc.)


Part VI: Storing Account Information


Now that you’ve successfully setup your account, you’ll receive documentation that contain important information related to it. Ideally, keeping this in a safe place (e.g. - lock, safe deposit box, strong box, concealed file folder, etc.) would be best. Make sure people you trust only have access (if that) to this information, as the information may be abused or mishandled.


Moreover, memorizing some of the following could eliminate the need to worry about safe storage:


  1. Four-digit account PIN number

  2. Bank account number

  3. Social Security Number


Of course, if you believe that this information has fallen into the wrong hands, you can always inform your bank and they’ll “freeze” the account to prevent unauthorized usage.


Part VII: Conclusion


Congratulations! You’ve now completed all the necessary steps to officially open a bank account! With your new account(s), you’ll be able to do some of the following:


  1. Withdraw cash when needed from an ATM

  2. Write checks to pay for purchases

  3. Make deposits to add to account balances

  4. Configure and use online banking features

  5. Setup a direct deposit to make balance-maintaining simpler

  6. Use them as a basis to set up special accounts in the future (e.g. - linked accounts, opening a high-interest savings account, etc.)

  7. Much more!
