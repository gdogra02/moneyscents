Balance Sheets, Income Statements, and Cash Flow Statements


Financial Statements: Lesson 1


Introduction


Financial statements are formal records that tell us about the financial position, changes in financial position, and the performance of a company. The information gathered through interpreting these statements help the company and people affiliated with the company make good financial decisions. Each financial statement tells us something different about how a company is doing. In this lesson, you will be learning about three main financial statements: balance sheets, income statements, and cash flow statements.


Balance Sheet


A balance sheet, otherwise known as a ‘statement of financial position’, provides important information about a company’s assets, liabilities, and shareholder’s equity at any given point in time. In simple terms, assets are the items of value owned by a company, liabilities are the debts that a company owes (claims against a company’s assets), and shareholder’s equity is a company’s net worth after their liabilities have been subtracted from their assets. A balance sheet always follows the accounting equation:


Assets = Liabilities + Shareholder’s Equity

The information found on a balance sheet is especially useful for investors as it is a quick way for them to have an idea of a company’s financial condition along with what a company owns and what they owe. However, since a balance sheet is just a snapshot of how a company is doing at a particular time, they would need to compare it with balance sheets from previous periods if they wanted to see how a company has been doing over a long period of time.


Figure 1 shows Netflix’s balance sheet which includes information from four quarterly fiscal periods. As you can see, a balance sheet is divided into three sections: assets, liabilities, and shareholder’s equity. There are many subsections within each section. Assets is split into two categories: current assets and long-term assets. Current assets are assets that can be liquidated within one fiscal year while long-term assets refer to assets that stay in a company for longer than a year.


On a balance sheet, assets are listed in order of liquidity (how quickly they can be converted into cash) and liabilities are put in order of which are more likely to be paid back first.


The total assets and the total liabilities plus the total stockholder’s equity must balance, hence why this is called a ‘balance sheet’. As shown in Figure 1, in the 2nd quarter, the total assets ($16,517,223) is equal to the total liabilities ($13,404,723) added to the total stockholder’s equity ($3,112,500).


$16,517,223 = $13,404,723 + $3,112,500

Income Statement


An income statement shows a company’s financial health over a particular accounting period. This type of financial statement contains three important pieces of information about a company: revenues, expenses, and net income. Revenue is the income earned from the sale of goods and services, expenses refer to the money used to run the business, and net income is the profit made after the expenses have been taken into account. A company’s net income is calculated using the following equation:


Net Income = Revenues - Expenses

The data in this type of financial statement contains valuable information. For instance, if you were looking to invest in a company, you would be able to see an overview of how profitable the company is just by looking at the company’s income statement. Using this information, you can determine whether or not investing in the company is worth it.


Two different types of activities can be found on an income statement: operating and non-operating. Within the operating section, you have the revenues and expenses earned through regular day-to-day business operations. For example, the transaction of a shoe company selling a pair of shoes would go under this section. Meanwhile, the non-operating category consists of revenues and expenses obtained through activities not directly tied to a company's regular operations. An example of this would be the same shoe company losing money from investments.


An income statement may look intimidating at first but don't worry, it is actually very straightforward. The calculation of the net income begins at the top of the income statement. Let's walk through an example.


The income statement of Apple Inc. is shown in Figure 2. This income statement contains information from four accounting periods. If we take a look at 9/24/2016, it starts by calculating the gross profit (total revenue - cost of revenue). Next, all of the operating expenses get subtracted from the gross profit. Finally, the tax is deducted from that number which leaves us with a net income of $45,687,000.

Cash Flow Statement


Similar to income statements, cash flow statements reports the amount of cash and short-term investments flowing in and out of the company. The major difference between the two is a cash flow statement does not include cash that has been recorded on credit.


There are three ways by which cash enters and leaves an enterprise. These three ways are analysed to determine the net cash flow.


  1. Operations: This includes changes that need to be made based on a business’ day-to-day operations. Accounts that are taken into consideration in this category are depreciation, adjusting entries, accounts receivable, accounts payable, inventory, and liabilities, just to name a few.

  2. Investing: Changes that are required depending on a company’s investments. A few examples of investments that would be in this category are capital expenditures, equipment, investments, and short-term assets.

  3. Financing: Adjustments made after taking into account a company’s debt, loans, and dividends.


A cash flow statement is useful when trying to determine how a company’s cash flow will be in the future. The information gathered from this helps companies plan their budget for the following year(s). This financial statement also gives an overview of the financial health of a company. The greater the net cash flow, the more money that is available for business operations.


Figure 3 shows an example of a cash flow statement taken from McDonald's and includes data from four fiscal periods. Starting with the net income, adjustments are made accordingly based on the operations, investing, and financing done by the company throughout the period. The amount that remains is the net cash flow.

Conclusion


And with that, you have reached the end of the lesson! In this lesson, you’ve learned about balance sheets and how they provide a snapshot of a company’s assets, liabilities, and shareholder’s equity. You also learned about income statements and their role in giving an overview of the financial health of an enterprise through revenues, expenses, and net income. Finally, you learned about the amount short-term investments and money coming in and out of a business shown through cash flow statements.
