Importance of Financial Literacy For Today's Youth


January 28, 2017 by Roger Nath


“Hey dad, what’s an RESP? What’s the difference between a chequing and savings account? What’s the difference between a credit and debit card?”


These are fundamental questions that many high-school students across Ontario, including myself, have asked in recent times. Moreover, from personal experience, I know that there are some who have no such questions or concerns, not because they already know the answers but due to an utter lack of exposure…


Financial illiteracy is an issue that stifles the potential for financial growth, independence, and a higher quality of life for many Canadian adolescents transitioning into adulthood. Before we go on, let’s examine some statistics which will provide an objective idea of the true scope of the issue. According to a 2014 study by the Investor’s Education Fund:


-Eight in ten young Canadians (ages 15-24) aren’t confident in their financial knowledge


-69% of high school students think it is important that schools teach them more about personal finance


-Only 3 out of 10 high-schoolers are actively saving for their education or other expenditures


-54% of teenagers indicated that they would not pay their credit card off in full each month


-39% of teenagers ranked “how to save money” as the most important topic to learn about; ranked second (for 20 per cent of students) was “how to use bank accounts”


Indeed, the statistics above show us the cold, hard truth on the matter: we unfortunately have a gap in financial literacy in our high-school curriculum.



The current state of financial literacy in the Ontario curriculum merely involves optional text-book usage in electives such as ‘Mathematics for Work and Everyday Life, Grade 11, Workplace Preparation (MEL3E)’, and ‘Section B: Personal Finance of the ‘Foundations for College Mathematics, Grade 12, College Preparation (MAP4C)’ course.


The two core problems with financial literacy in the current curriculum are: a) many of the current courses are electives that many students may not be able to take (not mandated); b) only surface-level material is taught in these electives (e.g. simple and compound interest).



In addition, organizations and companies such as MoneyScents, Proliteracy.ca, the Canadian Foundation for Economic Education, the Investor Education Fund, and Junior Achievement of Central Ontario hold intermittent workshops and programs on financial literacy. While many agree that these are certainly useful, they might not be enough to fill the current educational gap in financial literacy entirely.


That being said, more and more action has been taken on the issue as of late. For instance, The Toronto Youth Cabinet (TYC) recently put forth a petition to reform the ‘Grade 10 Careers Course’, implementing various aspects of financial literacy into the curriculum.



Their proposal included overarching topics related to earning money, saving money, investing, borrowing money, spending money, taxes, the economy, insurance, and protecting one’s money. Each topic would be segmented into sub-topics, and elaborated on heavily in the classroom through various activities (interactive quizzes, lessons, readings and presentations, etc.) similar to a regular lesson in a course like math or history.


Fortunately, the Minister of Education, Mitzie Hunter, responded well to the petition. In a formalized press conference (as well as a nice, short video message on twitter), she confirmed that the ministry in conjunction with the TYC are in the process of creating a module entirely dedicated to financial literacy in the grade 10 curriculum.


The grade 10 curriculum in particular is being targeted, as many agree it’s the ideal time for most students to begin developing financial literacy. They might need to manage an allowance (as I do), start working part-time to help pay for college, are only a few years away from managing lines of credit, and so much more.



As a highschool student myself, however, I think the ultimate solution will be reached when the government and TYC partner with specialized organizations like Proliteracy.ca (particularly digital platforms for the tech-saavy students in the 21st century) to deliver financial literacy content incorporated into their new module in school curriculums.


While learning how to factor quadratic trinomials or recite Shakespearean sonnets might be beneficial, many agree that the curriculum should place additional emphasis on teaching practical, directly-applicable-to-real-life skills and information, such as financial literacy.


While the ‘buck’ doesn’t stop here for boosting financial literacy (excuse the pun), hopefully, this new module and similar undertakings will be a step in the right direction.


With continued progress, Ontario’s financially literate students can become financially literate adults, leading to greater potential for financial growth, independence, and higher quality of life overall going forward.
