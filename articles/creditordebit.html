Credit or Debit Card: Which Should You Swipe?


Published on August 25, 2017 by Tiffany Tran


We live in a century where technology plays a major role in our daily lives. From Edison’s Light Bulb to The Internet to something as advanced as Gene Therapy, we have certainly come a long way. One of the many great things technology has brought us is digital currency, something we all use and rely on.


Digital currency differs from physical currency as it allows for quick and easy transactions. Two major ways we use digital currency to perform transactions is through credit cards and debit cards. Their greatest benefit? Convenience. Instead of having to carry around bills and coins (which although are small, they always seems to weigh a ton), all you need is a 8.6cm by 5.4cm plastic card. Both types of cards are very similar physically wherein they both have 16-digit numbers, the cardholder’s name, and the expiration date on the front side.



When you use a credit card as your method of payment, you are essentially borrowing money from your credit card issuer to pay. This sum of borrowed money must then be paid back in full within the grace period (usually 21 days after you have received your bill) to avoid having to pay interest. Piling up on credit card debt is something you do not want to do as the high interest rates associated with credit cards can leave you in deep financial trouble.


Credit cards are advantageous when it comes to resolving card theft and rewards and cashback. As debit cards are connected directly to your bank account, anyone that steals your debit card information will immediately have access to and use the money in the account. In addition, if you do not report the fraud within a certain period of time, you could be held liable for your loss. On the other hand, you have much longer to report credit card fraud and the money is taken from the credit card issuer, not directly from your bank account. This makes the charges easier and quicker to dispute than debit card fraud.



Many credit card issuers offer rewards and cashback programs as an incentive for you to use their credit card. For example, using a President's Choice Financial® Mastercard® will earn you points that can be redeemed for free groceries. Though some banks offer rewards for using their debit card, they are not as good as credit card reward programs as banks receive less than credit card companies for each transaction.



Debit cards, as mentioned above, are connected directly to your bank account. When you pay for something using this type of card, money is subtracted from your account. With debit cards, you do not have to worry about paying interest. However, you need to ensure that you do not spend more than you have in your bank account. Doing so will result in something called an overdraft charge. Overdraft charges tend to be high and acquiring these charges regularly could take a toll on your wallet.


With regards to lower overspending risks and better credit history, debit cards come out on top. The prospect of getting overdraft charges from the overspending of your debit card is a great way to stop you from doing so. Overdraft protection and rejection of transactions offered by your bank also prevents you from spending more than you have. Contrarily, credit cards allow you to “spend now and pay later” which will cause you to spiral into a pile of debt if you do not pay your bills on time.


Having good credit history is crucial. When investing in a car or a house in the future, the amount of interest you pay on the loans and mortgages depends on how good your credit history is. Insurance companies and potential employers also take a look at your credit history. Not paying your credit card bills on time impacts your credit history negatively while fully paying your bills on time will positively affect it. Meanwhile, how you use your debit card has no effect whatsoever on your credit history.


As you can see, credit and debit cards both have their advantages and disadvantages. With credit cards, not paying your bills on time results in compounded interest. Nonetheless, they are better regarding resolving card theft and rewards and cashback. Spending more than you have when using a debit card will land you overdraft charges but in terms of not overspending and having a good credit history, debit wins.


Whether you swipe your credit or debit card at the cash register depends on how you use your money and the lifestyle you live. No matter what you choose, as long as you pay your bills and do not spend more than you have, your financial future will be bright.


We all can agree that investing isn’t easy. We all make mistakes. But by thinking independently and understanding the behavioral pitfalls of many investors, the rational investor – who is willing to be contrarian when the facts bear out – and ride the wave when appropriate, may stand a better chance of reaching his or her investing goals.
