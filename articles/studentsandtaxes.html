Students and Taxes: What Can You Deduct?


May 30, 2017 by Roger Nath


As teenagers in high-school, many students are actively engaged in or pursuing employment opportunities like jobs, paid internships, and others. Naturally, this raises the questions: are these students subject to tax liabilities based on the income they earn from theses sources?


In short, it depends on their unique situation. It’s important to recognize that the CRA sets forth specific criteria applicable to all Canadians, regardless of age. However, there are special criteria that are more relevant to students. Moreover, certain tax issues are applicable to students and each tax issues has to be dealt with independently.


For instance, some students, depending on their unique situations, may be able to receive a tax credit to help offset some of their liability. Moreover, a high-school student’s income tax is based on the amount of taxable income that was earned in the previous year, and this obviously varies from student to student (not unlike with adults).


So, for more specificity, what are some reasons under which high-school students may file? According to the Services for Youth, some of the reasons include the following:


  1. One is requested to file by the Canadian Revenue Agency (CRA).

  2. One has employment insurance benefits that need to be paid.

  3. One needs to repay money to their registered retirement savings plan (RRSP) for a withdrawal related to a lifelong learning plan.

  4. One receives money from the working income tax benefit and would like to do so again in the following tax year.

Furthermore, you may be wondering in more detail what some of these tax-liable income sources are for students, which include some of the following as per the CRA:


  1. Income received from registered education savings plans (RESPs)

  2. Apprenticeship grants and training allowances

  3. Interest accrued from investment accounts

  4. Employment income reported by employers on T4 slip

  5. Amounts (partial or total) of scholarships, bursaries, etc., depending on the circumstances


As a high-school student/parent, one may think that it may be silly or unnecessary to report these incomes. However, one may be liable for penalties and interest levied by the CRA, if not careful or negligent about their tax situation.


Moreover as touched upon before, by disclosing one’s income on a tax return, one may actually be able to to receive certain tax credits and possibly even a refund.


In terms of deductions, high-school students are capable of claiming deductions applicable to any other Canadian tax-filer (i.e.- akin to adults), as long as they are eligible. Some benefits, credits, and deductions are more common to students thought.


For instance, consider that under certain circumstances, one’s tuition may be deductible if the course taken enhances their skills geared for a particular occupation, and the educational institution providing the course has been approved by Employment and Social Development Canada.


Many consider the topic of taxes to be boring and even downright unattractive; likely especially true for students. However, understanding the nuances of how they work (of course in addition to paying them… as the great Ben Franklin once said they are an inevitability after all) can be a particularly essential component of financial literacy for students and adults alike.


For more tax information, visit the following site provided by the CRA: http://www.cra-arc.gc.ca/ndvdls-fmls/menu-eng.html.
