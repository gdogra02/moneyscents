Introduction to Taxes


Taxes: Lesson 1


Introduction


I’m sure you have gone through a moment in your life where you are sitting in math class and wondering when you would ever need to know how to find the derivative of (insert complicated function here) in real life. You may have wondered why you aren’t learning about how to open a bank account or save money instead. Well, today is your lucky day because you will finally be learning about a concept that will definitely be useful outside of the classroom: taxes. In this lesson, you will be learning about what taxes are, the levels of taxation, and the types of taxes found in Canada.


What are Taxes


Let’s start off with the basics. What exactly are taxes? By definition, a tax is a compulsory fee that you are required to pay to the government. Programs and services such as public schools, libraries, roads, utilities, healthcare, and more may seem “free”, but they are actually funded by the government through the taxes that you pay, or at least the taxes that your parents pay.


There are three levels of taxation: federal, provincial/territorial, and municipal. Each level of taxation collects different types of taxes and uses the money collected for different purposes.


Federal Tax


Federal tax is collected through the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA). The CRA is in charge of administering tax laws for the Government of Canada, the majority of the provinces and territories, and manages different social and economic incentive programs found in the tax system.


The federal government is responsible for dealing with matters that affect the whole country. Supporting Canadians through Old Age Security and Employment Insurance, giving money to Provincial and Territorial Governments, Security, Infrastructure, and reducing Canada’s public debt are just a few of the many things the federal government spends our tax dollars on.


The CRA’s main job is to collect federal income tax. We will be going more in-depth into this type of tax later on.


Provincial/Territorial Tax


Provincial/territorial tax is obtained through a province/territory’s finance or revenue departments. A provincial/territorial government is responsible for services such as health care and education.


The main type of tax collected by the provincial/territorial government is consumption tax, a tax on the goods and services provided by a business. As you can imagine, the money collected is put towards funding the services listed above. Provincial/territorial governments also receives funding from the federal government, with the majority of the money coming from income taxes.


Municipal Tax


Finally, municipal taxes are collected through the area’s local government. A municipal refers to a city or a town. Toronto, for example, is a municipal. A municipal government is in charge of local services such as public libraries, hydroelectric systems, public transportation, parks, forestry, and recreation.


You have probably heard of the term property tax before. Property tax is collected by the municipal government. Similar to federal income tax, municipal income tax is also a type of income tax that is required in some municipalities.


Types of Taxes


The taxes that you are required to pay are broken up into many different categories. We will be going over three of the most common types: income taxes, consumption taxes, and property taxes.


Income Taxes

Income taxes are a tax on the money that you earn. Once a year, if you are earning more than $3,500 through an employer or self-employment, you are required to file a tax return. A tax return allows you to state your taxable income, tax credits, and deductions to see how much income tax you owe.


The amount of income tax you have to pay depends on several factors. The amount of employment income you make, where you live, and deductions you are eligible to declare are just a few. As stated above, income taxes are collected by the CRA and the majority goes to the federal government.


Tax brackets are divisions at which taxable income (the amount of income that will be taxed) changes. The higher a person’s income, the more they will be taxed. For example, imagine that there are three tax brackets of 5%, 10%, and 20%. The rate of 5% applies to income from $1 to $5,000; 10% applies to income from $5,001 to $15,000; and 20% applies to income above $15,001. If Person A earns $500, they would have to pay $25 (0.05 x 500) in taxes. If Person B earns $10,000, they would have to pay $250 (0.05 x 5000) for the first $5000 and $500 (0.1 x 5000) for the $5000 in the second bracket. The total amount of tax Person B pays is $750.


Consumption Taxes

You are probably most familiar with consumption taxes as it is the tax you deal with most on a daily basis.


Harmonized Sales Tax (HST), is a type of consumption tax that most Canadians are usually required to pay when purchasing goods or services. HST is used in provinces where the Provincial Sales Tax (PST) and Federal Goods and Services Tax (GST) has been combined. In Ontario, HST is 13%. 5% goes to the federal government and the remaining 8% goes to the provincial government. The percentage of HST varies from province to province.


Property Taxes

Anyone who owns a property has to deal with property taxes. Property taxes are usually calculated using the following formula:


Estimated property tax = Assessed Value x Residential Tax Rate

An assessed value is essentially a price (in dollars) that is attached to a property and is only used for the calculation of property tax. Each type of property has a different tax rate attached to it. For example, the residential tax rate for industrial properties is greater than the tax rate for residential properties.


Conclusion


Taxes, though not everyone’s favourite thing in the world, are an essential part of our society. Without them, you would not have access to many of the government run programs and services that exist today.


On a balance sheet, assets are listed in order of liquidity (how quickly they can be converted into cash) and liabilities are put in order of which are more likely to be paid back first.


Congratulations on completing this lesson on taxes! In this lesson, you learned about the three levels of taxation: federal, provincial/territorial, and municipal. You also learned about what taxes are used for and finally, you learned about different types of taxes and their purposes.
